Ralph Rapson: Sixty Years of Modern Design

MIA Exhibition March 27 - July 25, 1999
Weisman Exhibition March 27 - May 22, 1999

The Minneapolis Institute of Art and the Weisman Art Museum honor Ralph Rapson, one of the leaders in modern design and architecture, with the first retrospective exhibition of his most significant projects. Rapson's designs contributed to the evolution of modern style which began in the late 1930s and gained momentum in the years following World War II. The MIA's installation focuses on Rapson's numerous residential design projects, many of which are located in the Twin Cities. The Weisman Art Museum will feature Rapson's public commissions such as the Guthrie Theatre and the urban planning project Cedar-Riverside. Combined, the two installations contain over 250 architectural elements, models, furniture, photographs and drawings, many on display for the first time.

At The MIA, an introductory gallery will be devoted to his many designs for furniture and decorative arts. The MIA will recreate a portion of Rapson's modern design shop, Rapson, Inc., which he ran in Boston around 1950 with his wife, Mary. This section will also display Rapson's lively designs for tableware, flatware, jewelry and furniture. Subsequent sections of the exhibition will feature groups of Rapson's important experimental house designs, such as his Cave House (1938) and his Greenbelt House (1945), which will highlight his use of unconventional spaces and his lively design process.

Of Rapson's later residential commissions, the most well-known is the Philip W. Pillsbury House, built in 1963 on Lake Minnetonka for the president of the Pillsbury Company. Because the house was demolished in 1997, it only exists in drawings, photographs, architectural elements and in a virtual tour on CD- ROM produced at the time of demolition. Visitors to the MIA will experience a three-dimensional display about the house, which is considered one of Rapson's finest homes.

Rapson began making his major contributions to modernism in the late 1930s and early 1940s during a period of study and work with Eliel Saarinen at the Cranbrook Academy of Art in Michigan. He entered housing competitions sponsored by publishers and manufacturers in which his designs promoted creative, affordable housing for the middle class which maximized space, light and play areas. Many of his entries, such as the "Greenbelt House" (1945) were efficient yet provided a sense of luxury and connection to the outdoors through patios, an interior green space and large expanses of glass.

While head of the Department of Architecture at the Institute of Design in Chicago, Rapson collaborated with former Bauhaus designer László Moholy- Nagy on trade installations and new interiors for B&O Railroad passenger cars and with H.G. Knoll Associates on furniture designs. His involvement with Knoll eventually led to his designing the U.S. Embassy buildings in France, Denmark and Sweden.

During his thirty-year career as professor and head of the School of Architecture at the University of Minnesota (1954-1984), Rapson designed several nationally-significant examples of modern architecture. Among the most notable are the Tyrone Guthrie Theater in Minneapolis, the Philip W. Pillsbury Residence in Wayzata, Minnesota, and the award-winning Performing Arts Center at the University of California, Santa Cruz. Rapson is also responsible for the design of over 50 residences, numerous churches and several public buildings in Minneapolis. His commitment to better housing for city dwellers of all backgrounds led to the Cedar-Riverside project in Minneapolis, the first "New-Town-In-Town" sponsored by the U.S. Government's Department of Housing and Urban Development.
