Use the World of Art to Teach About History and Cultures

World Ceramics examines 12 ceramic objects from The Minneapolis Institute of Arts.

These on-line materials are:

  • Written for teachers outside the arts as well as art teachers.
  • Multicultural.
  • Interdisciplinary.
  • Divided into elementary, intermediate, and upper secondary sections. The teacher’s and upper secondary students’ sections are the same.
  • Supportive of the Minnesota Graduation Standards.

Concepts for Mastery include the following:

  • Interactions among people and cultures are reflected in ceramics.
  • Historical and cultural contexts provide meaning for ceramic works of art.
  • Environment influences the ceramics produced by a culture.
  • Symbols and ideas can be forms of personal and cultural expression.
  • Art has a variety of functions.
  • Ceramics techniques and processes can be used to create works of art.

Organization

Teachers can "Explore" World Ceramics via the Where in the World section.

  • When an object is selected, a small image appears with "Key Ideas" and topic headings. "Key Ideas" are the major aesthetic, historical, and functional concepts students are intended to learn. The information on these objects has been organized around these four topics:
    1. Where does it come from?
    2. How was it made?
    3. What does it look like?
    4. How was it used?
  • Two types of "Discussion questions" are also provided for each object:
    1. "Look" questions can be answered by close observation of the 12 on-line images.
    2. "Think" questions encourage analytical thinking that is based on observation.
  • "Additional Resources" such as bibliographic references and related materials from other web sites are also provided.

Throughout the text:

  • Glossary words are shown like this: VESSEL. When selected, a separate window pops up to reveal the definition.
  • Maps show geographical locations and relationships.
  • Stories that place the ceramic object in a historical and cultural context and make the piece more relevant to the student’s world accompany most of the objects.

ACTIVITIES FOR STUDENTS

For All Levels

Is There A Story?:

This section contains stories that place ceramic objects in their historical and cultural contexts.

Stories:

    1. Are illustrated and have audio.
    2. Are based on myths or folktales from the culture in which the ceramic object was made.
    3. Can be used as a storytelling presentation or as a self-directed student inquiry.
    4. Are written with students’ interests in mind.

How Big Is It?

Students can compare the sizes of some ceramic objects to a basketball or to each other.

How Do You Make It?

Students learn how ceramic objects were made by following step-by-step descriptions of these processes:

    1. Construction-
      • Coiling Method
      • Slab Method
      • Pinch Method
      • Method to Sand Mold a Medallion
      • Wheelthrown Method
    2. Surface Treatments-
      • Glazing
      • Incising
      • Sgraffito
      • Painting
    3. Clay and Firing Variations-
      • Earthenware
      • Stoneware
      • Porcelain

Elementary Student Activity (Grades K-5)

Can You Find...?

This activity revolves around a collage of 12 ceramic objects. One question at a time appears, addressing descriptive qualities of a ceramic object. Students answer questions by selecting an object in the collage. Whatever their answer, they will get a response. The responses guide the students’ search and teach additional concepts as students proceed through the activity.

  • For students in grades K-3 teachers can use the activity as a presentation to encourage student discussion.
  • For students in grades 4-5 teachers can use the activity in cooperative learning groups or as a self-directed inquiry activity.

(The activity is modeled on the Marzollo Wick "I Spy..." books, published by Scholastic, Inc.).

Learner objectives:

  • Acquire vocabulary related to the visual world.
  • Sharpen observational skills.
  • Carry out self-directed inquiry (when applicable).
  • Work in cooperative groups (when applicable).
  • Identify different ways cultures express themselves through art.

Intermediate Student Activity (Grades 6-9)

Who Knows...?

An archeologist, an artist, and an art historian tell what they see and think about when they examine ceramics from many different cultures. The student is taken on a journey of exploration that can be either an individual or a cooperative group experience. This activity, which combines role modeling and the constructivist teaching model, can be used as a tool for inquiry to accompany history, social studies, art, or language art lessons. A short assessment is included.

Learner objectives:

  • Expand vocabulary.
  • Enhance reading skills.
  • Sharpen observational skills.
  • Carry out self-directed inquiry.
  • Work in cooperative groups (when applicable).
  • Identify different ways cultures express themselves through art (symbols, style, decoration, etc.).
  • Learn about the work of artists, art historians, and archaeologists through role modeling.
  • Consider how a culture’s environment and resources affect the way people live.

Upper Secondary Student Activity (Grades 10-12)

Where In The World...?

This section is the same as the teacher’s section and can be utilized by the student as a tool for self-directed inquiry and research. Students find out where ceramic pieces came from and how ceramics fit into different cultures at different times in history.

Learner objectives:

  • Consider how historical events, geographical location, and cultural beliefs are reflected in works of art.
  • Understand the past and continuing development of ceramics as an art form.
  • Understand techniques and processes in creating ceramic objects.
  • Carry out self-directed inquiry and research.
  • Work in cooperative groups (when applicable).
  • Identify different ways cultures express themselves through art (symbols, style, decoration, etc.).
  • Consider how a culture’s environment and resources affect the way people live.
  • Expand vocabulary.
  • Enhance reading skills.
  • Sharpen observational skills.
