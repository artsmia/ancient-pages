The Minneapolis Institute of Arts celebrates Francis Bacon (1909 - 1992), a master of modern painting. Over 50 paintings and 18 triptychs illustrate the many dimensions of Francis Bacon’s imagery, from his notorious early crucifixion and seated pope themes to his lifelong preoccupation with distorted, postmodern human figures. Patrick Noon, Patrick and Aimee Butler Curator of Paintings and 20th Century Sculpture at The MIA, considers Bacon to be one of the premier painters of the twentieth century. Noon cites Bacon’s extraordinary, if disquieting, vision of the human condition in modern times and his sheer technical bravura as reasons for his appeal to contemporary audiences.

Bacon, born in Dublin to English parents, had no formal training as an artist. After earning a modest reputation in the 1920s as a modernist interior designer, he began painting in 1929. He first established himself as a major artist in 1944, when his now-famous triptych Three Studies for Figures at the Base of a Crucifixion was exhibited in London. Beginning in 1949, Bacon exhibited his work internationally and attracted attention from art critics in London, New York and Paris for his disturbing portrayal of figures distorted to such an extent that they appear to be turned inside out.

Bacon’s personal, expressionistic style continued to develop within the figurative tradition throughout the 1950s, even as the art world embraced abstraction. Major retrospectives at London’s Tate Gallery in 1962 and Paris’s Grand Palais in 1971 finally established Bacon’s pre-eminence as a modern master.
