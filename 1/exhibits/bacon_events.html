Film Series
Francis Bacon: Screen of Reference

Films are free for members and senior citizens. Adult and student tickets are $2.00; children 12 and under are 50¢. Films are shown in Pillsbury Auditorium. Please make reservations for groups of 12 or more by calling 612/870-3203.

Last Tango in Paris (1972)
Saturday, April 24, 2 p.m.

Bernardo Bertolucci used paintings by Bacon in the opening credits. Broader themes of raw sexuality and alienation are contrasted with formal elements from Bacon’s work. (color, 129 min., NC17)

The American Soldier (1970)
Saturday, May 8, 2 p.m.

German director Rainer Werner Fassbinder saluted Hollywood’s gangster film genre in this story of a charismatic hit man who completes his assigned murders with no hint of emotion or remorse. In German with English subtitles.
(b/w, 80 min., not rated)

Battleship Potemkin (1925)
Saturday, May 22, 2 p.m.

Sergei Eisenstein’s depiction of the mutiny of the crew of the Potemkin during the insurrection of 1905. Bacon’s interest in this film stemmed from the use of the scream as the ultimate expression of terror and he often worked from stills of this film. His obsession with the representation of raw meat can also be traced to the imagery found here. Silent with music track. (b/w, 74 min., not rated)

Un Chien Andalou (1928)
(b/w, 15 min., not rated)
L’Age D’Or (1930)
(b/w, 62 min., not rated)
Saturday, May 29, 2 p.m.

Bacon related his interest in surrealism to the work of filmmakers. These two films by Luis Buñuel attack organized religion and social order. Both are silent with a music track.
