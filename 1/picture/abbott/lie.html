Do Photographs Ever Lie?

According to Berenice Abbott and many other documentary photographers, the photograph should serve as an accurate document of the way things were when the picture was taken and as a record for the future. Abbott said, "Photography is to communicate the realities of life, the facts which are to be seen everywhere about us, the beauties, the absurdities, the achievements and the waste of contemporary civilization." To that end, she strove for ". . . acute and faithful presentation of what existed in the external world at a particular time and place." (Berenice Abbott, New Guide to Better Photography, 1953)

How faithful to her purpose was Berenice Abbott? On May 3, 1938 Abbott allowed a reporter from Popular Photography magazine to accompany her as she worked. Abbott drove to a rope store near the Brooklyn Bridge, which she had photographed before. On this day, she was interested in capturing the reflection of the bridge’s cables in the rope-store window. Her assistant set up her camera, loaded her film and chased away shoppers who wandered into the picture. Abbott asked the storekeeper to bring out more rope and a big winch "which shown like silver in the dull light." (Rosa Reilly, "Berenice Abbott Records Changing New York" Popular Photography, September 1938)

Abbott didn’t exactly stage scenes to photograph, but she stage-managed scenes she photographed, adding details, asking people to move away or hold a pose. For Abbott, making a documentary photograph as a historical record for the future was, in fact, always balanced with her desire to make a beautiful photograph.
