In contrast to documentary photography, the style that Berenice Abbott practiced and promoted, pictorial photography’s goal was to place photography on the same level as other fine arts like painting and sculpture. The pictorial movement, led by photographer Alfred Stieglitz and others, set out to make pictures that proved photographs were art. Berenice Abbott hated this notion. "What to me is anathema is for photography to be a bad excuse for another medium. Anything that smacks of painting, charcoal drawing, etching or aquatint is outmoded," she wrote in her book New Guide to Better Photography. "Let the medium speak for itself, frankly as what it is, the twentieth-century picture-making art."

Abbott often quoted fellow photographer Peter Henry Emerson, who wrote in 1886: "Avoid prettiness—the word looks much like pettiness, and there is but little difference between them." (Berenice Abbott, New Guide to Better Photography, 1953)

Berenice Abbott also had an opinion about Alfred Stieglitz and his circle of friends: "As far as I’m concerned he took about five good pictures in his whole life, and that was only when he ventured out of himself. He had nothing to do with me or my pictures. Everything had to revolve around him. It was one of the silliest and most outrageous cults I’ve ever seen. I’ve never liked any persons or schools that closed other people out." (Avis Bearman, Art News, January 1981)
