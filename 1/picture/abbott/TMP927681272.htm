New York in 1929

New York is wretched and opulent, with its countless tiny brick houses squatting beneath the marble palaces which house banks and industrial offices. But New York is the only city in the world rich enough in money, vitality, and men to build itself anew . . . the only city sufficiently wealthy to be modern.

—Bernard Fey, The American Experiment, 1929

The big event in 1929 New York was the October stock-market crash that set the stage for the Great Depression. But Berenice Abbott had arrived just in time to witness the birth of the New York skyscraper. The Chrysler building was finished in 1929, with the Empire State building and Rockefeller Center not far behind. No one in Europe had seen anything like these spiked towering symbols of modernism. Europe was old, New York was new, built by daring construction workers including Mohawk men hired for their strength, skill and fearlessness at heights that would leave others dizzy and gasping.

The Museum of Modern Art opened its doors in 1929. Prohibition was in effect, but that didn't stop "flappers" those modern, sassy young women in short dresses who smoked cigarettes and drank liquor in public. Jazz was the new music, created by such innovators as Louis Armstrong, Duke Ellington and Jelly Roll Morton. They produced what many consider to be America's greatest gift to music.

The Jazz Age in New York owed its beginnings to the fact that the Harlem Renaissance was in full swing in 1929. Throughout the 1920s, men and women from all over the country came together in Harlem to form an African American social and intellectual movement of terrific energy. Poets, writers, artists, dancers, playwrights, actors, photographers, musicians and more reaped the benefits of the thriving cultural center that was Harlem.

At the second annual Academy Awards (known as "The Oscars") in 1929, The Broadway Melody won "Best Picture." It was MGM's first full-length musical feature, advertised as "all-talking, all-singing, all dancing," including memorable hits such as "Give My Regards to Broadway." It was not surprising that a movie about Broadway received top honors. In 1929, New Yorkers attended plays on Broadway as often as people go to movies today.
Also new in 1929 were car radios and experiments on electronic color television. In 1929 Babe Ruth held the American League record for home runs. Berenice Abbott couldn’t fly to New York from Paris, but for the first time she could telephone New York from aboard her ship while crossing the Atlantic.


Berenice Abbott knew Eugene Atget for only a few months before he died, but from the moment she saw his photographs of Paris—streets, people, buildings and storefronts—she knew she had found something special. She bought Atget’s entire collection, more than 1,000 glass negatives and 7,000 prints, and brought them to the United States to promote them to museums, galleries, and art and photography magazines.

When Berenice Abbott arrived in New York in 1929 with Atget’s photos, she was planning on a three-week visit. She had been living in Europe for eight years, where she had an established and successful photography business. But what she saw in New York took her breath away. Unbelievable wealth and heart-breaking poverty; cars, trains and trolleys among horse-drawn milk carts; straight-sided skyscrapers soaring up around old ramshackle buildings; rectangles everywhere; an intense machine of a city. Abbott never returned to Paris. Instead she began photographing New York just as Atget had photographed Paris. She wanted to make a photographic record of this city of contrasts. But Abbott would photograph New York in her own way, imposing her love of facts and her belief that photography, a twentieth-century invention, was the only medium worthy of capturing twentieth-century New York. She set up a studio in Manhattan and spent the next ten years photographing New York.
