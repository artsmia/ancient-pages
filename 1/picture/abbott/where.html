Berenice Abbott meant to include pictures of people in her study of New York, but circumstances prevented her from completing her plan. In 1935, the federal government funded Abbott's project through the Works Progress Administration, enabling her to spend all of her time photographing New York. But the government never approved her purchase of a small, portable camera suitable for taking pictures of people on the street in live-action settings. Then, in 1938, the funding for her project was cut. In December of that year, as she was printing the final pictures for her survey of New York, she told an interviewer, "There are lots of things I could have taken in the last five years, if I only had better cameras … You want to get a crowd of people really protesting something. If you try to get the expression on their faces, you are stuck …" (Robert W. Marks, Coronet, December 1938)

Even though Abbott’s New York photographs seldom include figures they do include people in an indirect way. Abbott's New York was a city full of the things people made, thus reflecting peoples’ lives. As Art News writer Avis Berman pointed out, "Abbott's true theme was the secret life of inanimate things. [She] embraced an austere vision of life and… used light to reveal that the products of human ingenuity have a quivering presence, if not a superior personality, for those who care to seek them." (Avis Berman, Art News, January 1981)

Works Progress Administration (WPA)

The Works Progress Administration was formed in 1935. It consisted of projects like the construction of schools, parks and highways that the federal government used as a way to put people to work during the depression. The Federal Art Project, a small division of the WPA, gave Berenice Abbott a job. Her document of New York showed that art, as well as schools and parks and highways, contributed to the good of the country and its people.
