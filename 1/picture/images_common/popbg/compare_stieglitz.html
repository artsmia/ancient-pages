In contrast to documentary photography, the style that Berenice Abbott practiced and promoted, pictorial photography’s goal was to place photography on the same level as other fine arts like painting and sculpture. The pictorial movement, led by photographer Alfred Stieglitz and others, set out to make pictures that proved photographs were art, not just tools that real artists (painters) used to remember what their subjects looked like. Berenice Abbott hated this notion. "What to me is anathema is for photography to be a bad excuse for another medium. Anything that smacks of painting, charcoal drawing, etching or aquatint is outmoded," she wrote in her book New Guide to Better Photography. "In fact, trying to make a photograph look like an etching is just as bad as the fallacy in architecture of building a skyscraper with steel frame construction and then covering up the basic form with pseudo-Gothic ornament. Let the medium speak for itself, frankly as what it is, the twentieth-century picture-making art." Abbott often quoted fellow photographer Peter Henry Emerson, who wrote in 1886: "Avoid prettiness—the word looks much like pettiness, and there is but little difference between them." (Berenice Abbott, New Guide to Better Photography, 1953)
