Rwanda 1994


While the photograph of Bosnia on the left details a cast of contemporary characters, involved in the Bosnian conflict and their relationships to each other, the photograph on the right makes refugees of the war in Rwanda look timeless, almost beautiful. Is Gilles Peress a witness to the situations he photographs, or an artist making pictures that ask more questions than they answer?

According to critic and photographer Martha Rosler, photojournalism “is concerned with the . . . presentation of social and historical situations and events. Marks of the creator are secondary.” Art photography, on the other hand, is “centrally concerned with the photographer’s sensibility,” even though the photograph may contain the same subject matter as the photojournalist’s photo. To put it another way, the photojournalist strives to keep his or her personality out of the picture, presenting an “objective” view of places and events; the artist wants us to look through his or her eyes, inviting us to experience the photographer’s opinions or feelings about the subject. Rosler points out, “the confusion between journalism and art and the increasing speed of the conversion from one to the other . . . characterize our age.” (Martha Rosler, lecture at Walker Art Center, 1981)

Both of these photographs and much more of Gilles Peress’ work (188 photos in all) are in the collection at The Minneapolis Institute of Arts. Art museums play a role in deciding what’s art and what’s not, depending on what they collect. The fact that an art museum collects the news of the day as seen in Peress’ photos suggests that the concept of art museums is changing too. “Art is a much more elastic term than it was,” points out Stephen Crowley of the New York Times, a publisher of much of Peress’ work. This change in the character of art museums is “partly because of photography, which by nature makes the traditional definitions [of art] quaver.” (Stephen Crowley, New York Times Sunday Magazine, April 17, 1994)
