The Photo League was formed in the 1930s in New York by a group of photographers who wanted to document events of social significance that were not reported in the mass media. Rejecting other popular forms of photography like sentimental portraits or pretty landscapes, these photographers were interested in ordinary working people and the conditions under which they lived. Jerome Liebling joined the League when he was a 22-year-old photography student at Brooklyn College. The League’s statement of beliefs read, in part: “Photography has tremendous social value. Upon the photographer rests the responsibility and duty of recording a true image of the world as it is today. The Photo League’s task is to put the camera back in the hands of honest photographers, who will use it to photograph America.” (Documentary Photography, 1972) The unwritten part of The Photo League’s beliefs was that its members would also use photography to promote social change.

The Photo League published a free newsletter, Photo Notes. The League also ran a school where students paid $30 for a 12-session semester of classes on the history, techniques and philosophies of photography. Students were never turned away if they couldn’t pay. The Photo League and its members soon became known all over the country as students graduated and traveled, making pictures and carrying The Photo League’s philosophy to other communities outside of New York City.

“I thought of photography as a means of self expression,” wrote League member Lou Bernstein, “and the League’s philosophy suited me because it emphasized the expression of personal viewpoints, even in the photographs we took of social conditions. I think that’s an attitude that is missing in almost all of the photography schools today; there should be an institution that encourages young photographers to express themselves.” (Lou Bernstein, Documentary Photography, 1972)

The Photo League disbanded in the late 1940s after its members were criticized by the Department of Justice for having Communist sympathies. A few members of The Photo League were accused of being members of the Communist Party. Some photographs, because they championed common people and frequently exposed social injustice, were interpreted as critical of America’s values and its government. For a time membership grew as other photographers joined the League to show their support. In the end, however, most members realized that their careers were in peril and decided to resign.
