Ted Hartwell, Curator of Photography at the Minneapolis Institute of Arts, was invited to Indianapolis, Carl Pope’s hometown, to judge a photography competition. Pope had not entered his work in the competition; he just showed up and asked Hartwell to take a look at his pictures. He hadn’t submitted his photographs for the competition because, he recalled, “I felt burnt out about showing around Indianapolis and people used to say the most insulting things to me about my work. I felt they didn’t have any understanding of my work or the history of photography and I just quit showing my work around here.” After viewing hundreds of photos on his trip to Indianapolis, Hartwell purchased only Pope’s for the museum. “It was one of the first institutions that ever collected my work,” remembers Pope.

Hartwell purchased four of Pope’s photographs for the collection at the Institute, and Pope gave the museum another four. “I wanted to donate the others because I wanted them to stay all together,” said Pope. “I wanted the museum to have a set. Even though I was in my twenties I already understood the history of photography and the importance of my work being conserved.”

Any artists whose work lives in a museum’s collection can be certain that it will be well preserved. The Minneapolis Institute of Arts stores photographs in climate-controlled rooms, sealed from dust and light which could damage delicate paper. In fact, as Pope knew, most artists aren’t able to care for their work as well as a museum can.

The purchase of Pope’s work turned out to be a great deal for both the photographer and the museum. Pope knew the Institute would preserve his work for the future, and Hartwell was pleased to get eight photographs for the price of four. “The price was real low,” recalls Pope, “and I still try to keep it low for institutions in this early part of my career. Even though I am 38 years-old I still feel like it is early.”
