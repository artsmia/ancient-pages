“Gilles Peress is not stuck in conventional ways of composing a picture,” says Minneapolis Institute of Arts Photography Curator Ted Hartwell, “so surprising things can happen. Carl was doing the same thing with that little camera.”

Implied lines in photographs can happen when people’s eyes are focused in a certain direction or when their paths of movement seem to suggest a route or line through a picture. The photographers who made these photos were working worlds apart, one in Carbondale, Illinois, and one in Azerbaijan, Iran, but they both understood the power of implied lines. As viewers we naturally follow the attention of the people in the picture–we want to see what they see or are headed toward. In both of these photos gazes and movement create a network of implied lines that intersect throughout the pictures, a web that holds them together. They both even have a line that leads directly to us. In one picture, it is the gaze of one eye looking straight into the camera. In the other picture it is the subject’s direction–if he continues on the path that he has taken in the picture, he will run right into the photographer/viewer.
